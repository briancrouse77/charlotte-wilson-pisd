Her Platform

Five Things She'll Fight For — Starting Day One

These aren't campaign promises invented for a flyer. These are classroom realities Charlotte witnessed as a PISD teacher. Every single one is actionable.

⚖️

Campus Equity — No More Two-Tier Schools

Princeton's campuses may share a name, but they don't share equal resources, staffing, or support. Charlotte will audit the gap between campuses and demand a real plan to close it — because your child's zip code shouldn't determine the quality of their education.

🎓

Real Support for Special Ed & Bilingual Learners

These students are legally entitled to qualified staff and proper resources. Right now, they're getting waiting lists and workarounds. Charlotte will hold the board accountable for measurable improvements — with timelines and public reporting, not promises.

🔎

Independent Audit — Follow the Money

Where are Princeton taxpayer dollars actually going? Are technology contracts competitive? Are special programs being delivered? Charlotte is calling for an independent, public audit — because a board that won't allow scrutiny has something to hide.

📋

Open Records, Open Board

Meeting minutes from 2022–2024 were not easily accessible to residents when Charlotte raised the issue. That's not how democracy works. She will push for full public access to every vote, every contract, every decision — no exceptions.

🤝

Community First — 365 Days a Year

Most board members disappear between elections. Charlotte commits to showing up — to PTOs, faith communities, neighborhood meetings, and local businesses — not just in October before a vote, but every month of her term.

Accountability Watch

Princeton Asked. The Board Didn't Answer.

Charlotte raised specific, documented concerns in the 2025 campaign. These weren't rumors — they came from public records and candidate forums. The board still hasn't given straight answers. Princeton deserves better.

🏛️

TASB Out. TEE & TFER In. Who Decided?

Princeton ISD quietly stopped working with the Texas Association of School Boards (TASB) — the established, widely-trusted state organization — and moved toward Texans for Excellence in Education (TEE) and Texas Foundation for Education Rights (TFER).

These newer groups have been linked to charter school expansion advocacy. The shift happened with zero public community input. At the 2025 candidate forum, the board compared it to choosing between "Coke and Pepsi" — an answer that dodges the real question entirely.

Still unanswered: Who authorized this change? When? What was the community's role? What financial or philosophical commitments came with it?
📄

Three Years of Missing Meeting Minutes

During her 2025 campaign, Charlotte flagged that PISD board meeting minutes for 2022, 2023, and 2024 were not publicly accessible to residents. Under the Texas Open Meetings Act, this is a legal obligation — not a suggestion.

Public school boards make decisions about your children, your tax dollars, and your community. Those decisions must be on the record. If they're not — that's the story.

You can act right now: Submit a public records request directly to PISD at open-records-request@princetonisd.net. Then share what you find.
Note: These are issues raised during the documented 2025 campaign and covered in local reporting. Charlotte's campaign presents them as open community questions — not established conclusions. Residents are encouraged to research independently and engage directly with the district.
